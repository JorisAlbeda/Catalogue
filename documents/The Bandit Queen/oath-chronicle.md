# Oath Chronicle

The old Chancellor — the Sorcerer of the Woods — had been gone half a year by the time the Cradle began deciding, on its own, who'd hold what he'd left behind. Not dead, not so far as anyone could actually prove; he'd simply handed Lilianne the throne not long after her last birthday and walked out of Deep Woods into no account of him since, and her mother Naiad still spoke of him, when she spoke of him at all, in a tense that left the door open. The Old Oak had marked the occasion with a gift the same week — an orb holding the last of the Fire Talkers' own art, a weapon nobody in the Cradle had wielded in living memory. Naiad called it a choice dressed up as a gift and didn't pretend to like it. Lilianne rode out anyway, her mother's Rangers at her back, chasing down old debts at Drowned City — Sticky Fire bought off men who remembered when the mill-ruins there ground grain instead of grudges, a Cracked Horn pulled from a doorway shut eighteen years. Sorrel Wick never left her desk. While the Chancellor's daughter gathered treasures like a girl proving she deserved a crown nobody had actually measured her for yet, the Truthwatcher of Charming Valley built quieter things: a light-fingered new hand at her table, a promise from the Forest Council that nobody hostile to her would find welcome there. "Flinching," Ossa called it. "Tuesday," she called it back. Two valleys over, Corvin Varn and Perrin Ashe found each other on the same patch of bandit-held ground within the same week, and settled, without discussing it, that neither of them would be the one to leave first.

Corvin won that ground and lost it to Perrin twice within a season. The second time, Perrin called him to the mill not to refight it but to ask an old, small question instead — what the iron key on Bettina Marsh's nail used to open, before the charter it belonged to went missing from the valley's own keeping. Corvin didn't know, and said so plainly, and somewhere in that plainness the two Exiles stopped measuring each other as rivals and started, almost without noticing, keeping each other's accounts instead. A season later Perrin stood at the fork in the river where his grandfather Halvard Cray had once sworn an oath the world broke on him, drew out the charter he'd carried two years without saying its name aloud, and told the companies gathered there that this had been Halvard's war, and it was his now. Nobody went home. Corvin, for his part, took to wearing a ring that had been his grandmother's — resized twice, because neither jeweler wanted to ask why a dead woman's ring kept needing to fit someone new — and by the war's last quiet night, sitting outside the mill with Perrin while Bettina's lamp burned down behind its shutters, he'd stopped pretending the ring was only ever about his grandmother at all. Neither of them mentioned it again after that night. It stayed still a good while longer than it had all evening.

Sorrel, meanwhile, spent her way into the one prize she actually wanted — the Banner of the People's Favor — and told Ossa, when he called it rich for a single day, that it was the only prize actually worth being poor for. And when Lilianne rode into Narrow Pass and came away with the old Bandit Crown, the bandit witch who'd ruled the pass for decades, Daz, knelt to her without being asked twice, the way people kneel to someone they've already decided is inevitable. From that day on, all bandit clans recognised the Chancellor as The Bandit Queen.

It was never really a war between strangers. In a tower above Drowned City's flooded ruins, Lilianne told her oldest friend, half to herself, that everything was starting to feel real now that her father was gone and Perrin had declared himself an enemy. Sorrel didn't offer her anything soft. "I came back here for you," she said. "Not against you." She meant it. It didn't stop what came next. Overheard admitting that very plan to her own people, Sorrel was struck from the Empire's rolls in Corvin's own hall, the banner changing hands a third time that same week — banished, paid off in favor she hadn't earned and didn't want, told to clear out inside three days. She didn't go far. She took Buried Giant from the bandits squatting it before the three days were even up, and bought her own exile's cause — the People's Favor — back with the same coin the Chancellor had just paid her to leave.

What Sorrel found next was older and stranger than any banner: a claim to the throne through blood and secret-keeping that nobody else at the table had thought to chase, sitting untouched since before any of them had sat down to this. She declared it outright. One dawn away from winning, by that same old law. Word reached Lilianne in time. She came with an army Sorrel had no answer for, and burned every soldier holding the ground long after there was any doubt who'd win. It wasn't a fight. It was a message: nothing gets rebuilt here. Whatever was left of the girl Sorrel had grown up beside went into that fire with the rest.

In the same week, Lilianne rode her python three regions to the Ruined Temple, where Halvard Cray had once held court before Perrin's family lost even the memory of the throne. She offered Perrin everything his grandfather had lost there — Fertile Valley restored, a seat at her own table, and, drawn from her Ranger's bag, the Horned Mask itself, the very relic the Sorcerer of the Woods had worn the day he broke Halvard on this same ground. "You will not be a pawn in my Empire," she told him. "You will be a symbol." Perrin didn't reach for it. He'd marched two companies into a bandit's own fire to end up holding real ground, he told her, not to end up a well-dressed ornament in her hall — and if he ever took that mask off anyone's hands, it would be because he'd won it, not because a Chancellor found it useful to hand it to him at the very ruin where his grandfather had lost it. She asked him, almost gently, what kind of man he wanted history to remember. "The kind who held this valley because he won it," he said, "not because a Chancellor found it useful to let him." Lilianne nodded, something almost satisfied in the refusal itself, as though it had told her exactly what she'd come to learn. "Then when next we meet, Lord Ashe, it shall be as enemies." She was gone west on the python's back before the sun had finished rising, and Perrin stood alone a long while in the broken hall, less sure than he wanted to be about which of them had actually won that morning.

He answered her the only way left to him: took Narrow Pass a third time, for good, and left nothing of hers standing east of it. Later, alone at the mill, he opened what his own grandfather had locked away decades before and finally found the truth of it — a letter admitting Halvard had spent his whole reign mistaking the valley for the thing worth keeping. Perrin folded it once, the same crease it already had, and put it back exactly where he'd found it. Some debts, once actually paid, don't need keeping.

When the reckoning finally came, there was no contest in it. Five relics, both banners, an empire that had never once been seriously in danger of leaving her hands — Lilianne of the Woods, eighteen years old when the mantle first fell too large on her shoulders, stood confirmed ruler of everything her father had held. It didn't feel like winning. Naiad found her alone in her tent that same night, hollow-eyed over what the Fire Talkers' own weapon had done to men who'd never had the chance to surrender, and made her say the rest of the sentence she'd started — _I don't like this_ isn't the whole of it. So Naiad told her, at last, what she'd never finished telling her before Drowned City: that Lilianne's father had stood in this exact silence once, that his own hand, not just the Fire Talkers', had struck the killing blow on Isolde Varn, and that history had simply let the wrong name carry it since. That he'd spent the years after hunting down whoever else had survived that day, growing quieter each time he came home from the searching, until an unmarked letter reached him a year ago — an answer to something he'd carried fifty-nine years — and he simply didn't come back from where it sent him. Whether he was still out there chasing it, nobody could say for certain. Lilianne sat with all of it — her own war, her father's, the space where an explanation should have been — and made the only decision that felt like hers alone: they would bury the Flame itself, raise a memorial over the ground it had burned, and she would swear a new Oath, built on the people's own well-being rather than a weapon. Naiad called it naive, in the only way worth being Chancellor for, and didn't argue against it.

Two months later, Sorrel walked into the one corner of the new country nobody had claimed — the Ruined Temple — with nothing left to rule and no wish to rule anything again, and found both her old rivals waiting. Corvin gave her the truth instead of comfort about an old debt she'd carried since Wren Sable died; Perrin gave her ground to pick, no claim attached. She took a night to decide, and a room at the mill instead of the road back out. A month after that, Corvin rode south to stand on the ground his own grandmother died on, and found Lilianne there too, overseeing the first stones of the memorial she meant to raise over the buried fire, on some private errand of her own besides. Over tea neither of them had poured, she told him what nobody living still held: that Isolde Varn had let an innocent man carry her own crime for thirty years, and only confessed it dying, when it cost her nothing anymore. Corvin left with a heavier debt and a lighter grudge. Lilianne left with a name to go looking for.

A year on, the mill wheel turns easier than it used to, and Corvin wears that ring on a cord around his neck instead of his finger, since neither he nor Perrin ever saw the point in forcing it. Sorrel sends word more often than she comes in person these days, running the pass her own way, answering to nobody's crown — but she's promised to come down before the frost, to finally collect on a room she never used. Whatever the reckoning cost each of them, none of them are paying it alone anymore.
