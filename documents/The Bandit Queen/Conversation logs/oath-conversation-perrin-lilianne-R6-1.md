# Perrin Ashe & Lilianne of the Woods — the Ruined Temple (Round 6)

Perrin reached the temple an hour early, on purpose. The stonework had gone soft at the edges the way old things do, moss thick in the letters of an inscription nobody living could read anymore, but the shape of the place was still unmistakably a hall — a throne room, once, back when this valley answered to a different name and a different family. His grandfather had ruled from somewhere near this exact spot, before the throne answered to someone else's charter instead of his. Perrin stood where the dais would have been and didn't sit, though something in him wanted to.

He sent his two loose companies to wait at the tree line, out of sight but not far, and told Fenn to stay quiet unless spoken to. Then he waited, turning the scar over his eyebrow with two fingers the way he did when a door wouldn't open right away, watching the road for a Chancellor who wasn't there yet.

"Halvard held court here once," he said, to no one in particular, or maybe to the ruin itself. "Let's see if his granddaughter's granddaughter thinks that's worth burning too."

A rumbling sounded over the mountains — the answer to a question Perrin hadn't quite let himself ask, how a week was time enough to cross from the Cradle through Narrow Pass to Fertile Valley at all. An enormous python crested the ridge and poured down into the valley, and beside the Ruined Temple it stopped, and two figures dropped from its head to the broken doorstep: a ranger, and a ruler. Her Excellency the Chancellor — Bandit Queen to the clans who'd named her that themselves, daughter of the very Sorcerer who'd bested Halvard — crossed the threshold with a face too young for the weight her bearing carried anyway.

"Perrin Ashe," she said. "We meet at last."

Perrin's eyes went to the python coiling itself along the broken wall, then back to her, unhurried. "You brought one soldier and something I can't count as a soldier at all," he said. "I'll call that keeping your word, Chancellor, same as your father kept his with my grandfather — right up until he didn't. Sit, or don't. Either way, tell me why I'm not already the next name on that thing's dinner list."

The ghost of a smile. "I assure you, Sir Ashe, you have nothing to fear from me here. We will have our talk, and we will leave here, as civilised men."

Perrin almost laughed at that — not unkindly. "Civilised is a word for what's left standing after, not for what anyone means going in. Corvin's still standing, and so's the woman you turned out of her own city with a purse for her trouble, so I'll grant you know how to be civilised when it suits you." He nodded once at the broken dais behind him, where a different family's throne had stood. "Say your piece, Chancellor. I've got a war on, and you're either in my way or you're not."

"Ah yes, this war of yours. Not going too well, is it? How do you expect to face the might of the Empire if a few bandits are so much trouble for you? Not to mention, your time is running out. You know this."

Perrin let that sit a moment before he answered it. "I know exactly how many rounds I've got left, same as you know exactly how many sites you're short of calling this valley yours outright — you wouldn't have ridden that thing three regions to tell a dying man he's dying. You came because the clock worries you too." He held her eyes. "So which is it — you're here to watch me lose, or you're here because you're not sure I will."

"I am here to make you an offer." She examined one of the few intact statues in the cracked hall, a relic of an age well before Cray's own reign. "You seek to further the conquest your grandfather once envisioned but never carried out. I will give it to you. Join the Empire, and help protect it — and I swear I will see Fertile Valley restored to its former glory."

Something in Perrin's chest went very still and very loud at once, and he hated her a little for knowing exactly where to aim. "You say that like you're the first person to offer me my own home back for the price of my name," he said, quiet now, no more needling in it. "My grandfather held this valley once without owing anyone a crown for it. I didn't come here to trade that debt to you instead of him." He looked at the statue she was studying, older than either of their houses. "What does 'help protect it' actually cost me, Chancellor — say it plain, and I'll tell you plain whether I can live with it."

"Let me speak plainly. Sorrel has begun a war. Her forces have overtaken Buried Giant, and she is now ready to trample the Empire with force. I will not see my civilians hurt for the sake of a rivalry." She looked at him squarely. "Stop throwing away your soldiers' lives fighting a few measly bandits. Commit your forces to the Empire's defence. Help fight back against Sorrel. That is what I'm asking of you. A battle actually worth fighting. What say you?"

Perrin turned that over slowly, and didn't like the shape of it any better the longer he held it. "You want my companies pointed at Sorrel so yours don't have to be — and you're calling that the battle worth fighting, over the one where I'm the one who actually stands to rule something after." He shook his head, more to himself than her. "I've got no love for a woman I've never met picking a fight with your civilians, but her war isn't my war, Chancellor, and every site I win still has to be one you don't hold when the count is taken — help you keep the six you've got, and my own clock runs out faster, not slower." He met her eyes again. "Tell me what's actually in it for Perrin Ashe, not for the Empire, and we'll see if there's a deal in this ruin after all."

She turned to her ranger. "Naiad?"

Naiad crossed to him and drew from her bag a sight to behold — the Horned Mask, the very one the Sorcerer of the Woods had worn the day he broke Halvard Cray. It commanded a kind of quiet even from the ruin itself. "You will realise the cultural significance of gifting this to you," Lilianne said. "You will not be a pawn in my Empire. You will be a symbol."

Perrin didn't reach for it. His eyes went from the mask to Naiad to Lilianne and stayed there, and when he finally spoke his voice had gone flat and careful, the way it did over something that might break if he raised it. "That's my grandfather's whole defeat you're holding out to me like a wedding gift, Chancellor, and I'm meant to wear it and call it honor." He made himself breathe once before he went on. "A symbol still only stands where somebody else plants it. I didn't march two companies into a bandit's own fire to end up a very well-dressed ornament in your hall." He looked at the mask a moment longer, and something in his face didn't quite match his own words. "Put it away. If I ever take that thing off your hands, it'll be because I won it, not because you handed it to me at a ruin with my grandfather's own name still carved somewhere in the walls."

She shook her head. "How many soldiers will you let die in waste for your vanity, Ashe? What will your vision really have meant if your battles never had a meaning? Fine — take a different relic from our Reliquary. The symbol stands."

Naiad turned as the python grew restless behind her; dawn was coming on. "Your Excellency —"

Lilianne nodded, once. "Yes, we must be off. Final offer, Ashe. What kind of man will you be remembered as?"

Perrin looked past her, to where the dais would have stood, and answered like a man who'd already had this argument with himself long before she ever arrived. "The kind who held this valley because he won it, not because a Chancellor found it useful to let him. My grandfather made your father's mistake once — mistook being given a place for actually holding it, and it cost him everything the day the two stopped meaning the same thing. I'm not making it twice, not even dressed up as a gift." He held out nothing, took nothing. "No relic, no symbol, no soldiers pointed anywhere but where I decide to point them. Tell your histories whatever they want about me when this is over, Chancellor — I'd rather be remembered as the man who lost trying than the one who won by standing where you put him."

Lilianne nodded, something almost satisfied in it, as if the refusal itself had told her what she'd actually come to learn. "Then when next we meet, Lord Ashe, it shall be as enemies. Give my regards to the Bandit Witch of the Narrow Pass." The hint of a smirk, and she turned; moments later the python carried them west, back the way the rumbling had come.

Perrin stood alone in the broken hall a while after the sound of it had gone, turning over that last, strange line — *Bandit Witch*, the name the Empire apparently had for Daz, though nobody at that table had called him anything but chief. He wondered, not for the first time, how much the Chancellor actually knew about the ground she'd just watched him lose twice. He finally called the two loose companies in from the tree line and started for home, already less sure than he wanted to be about which of them had actually won this morning.
