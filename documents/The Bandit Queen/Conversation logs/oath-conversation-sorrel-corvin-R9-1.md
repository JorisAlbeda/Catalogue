# Sorrel Wick & Corvin Varn — Four Months After the War

Corvin had ridden south from Ashvale a month after the Ruined Temple, following the letter she'd left with Bettina before dawn, meaning to find what Sorrel herself had admitted she didn't have the right to find on Wren's behalf. He came back three months later than he'd meant to, and the first thing he did before unpacking anything else was find Sorrel and tell her, plainly, what had happened at Drowned City — that he'd gone looking for a witness and found Lilianne there instead, investigating some private matter of her own; that the two of them had talked longer and easier than either had expected; that Lilianne had told him what her father once said of Isolde and Wren, confirming the harder truth Sorrel's own letter had only guessed at; and that, before he'd left, he'd told Lilianne some of what Sorrel had said about her in the weeks since the exile, and encouraged her to come find out for herself whether any of it still held.

Sorrel didn't say anything for a while after he finished — long enough that Corvin actually looked up from his tea to check she'd heard him at all.

"You told her," she said finally, not a question. "What I said. About there being nothing of Lily left." She turned that over, watching the fire rather than him. "I said that to you in a letter I sent you, Corvin. Not to her. Not for her." A beat, and when she went on it was flatter than accusing, more like arithmetic that hadn't balanced. "I don't get to take it back now that it's traveled. I just would've liked to be asked before it did."

She let that sit before she moved past it — she wasn't going to make him carry the whole visit for one line he probably hadn't weighed as heavily as she just had. "The rest of it, though." She exhaled, something between a laugh and not one. "Isolde let him take the blame for a crime that was hers. Lived it out as someone's shame instead of her own, for years, and only said so with her last breath, when saying so cost her nothing anymore." Her jaw tightened, just slightly. "That's not a debt. That's a theft with better manners than most. Wren spent thirty years not deciding if she deserved forgiveness for it, and I've spent this whole war asking the wrong half of that question out loud to anyone who'd sit still for it. Thank you for not letting me go on doing that."

She was quiet again, and this time when she spoke it wasn't about Isolde at all. "And Lilianne told you she's failed worse than her father did. Buried the fire. Calls herself the Bandit Queen now, like it's a joke she's still deciding whether to laugh at." Something crossed her face that wasn't quite hope and wasn't quite grief, closer to both refusing to leave the room at the same time. "I watched her burn every soldier I had rather than just win. That's not a woman who sounded sorry, the day it happened. But people don't usually confess to failing worse than their father unless something in them's actually cracked open a little." A pause. "I don't know what to do with that yet. I'm not going to pretend I do."

Last, and quieter: "You told her to find out for herself. Whether there's still something worth waiting on." She looked at him properly then, not unkindly, but plain. "That wasn't yours to offer either, Corvin. Same mistake, smaller size." Almost — almost — the corner of her mouth moved. "You're getting good at handing people questions that aren't yours to ask. I'd tell you to stop, except I did the exact same thing to you a season ago with a scrap of cloth, so I've lost the right to lecture you about it."

She didn't say whether she wanted Lilianne to actually come. She didn't say she didn't, either.
