# Corvin Varn & Lilianne of the Woods — Drowned City, a month after the Ruined Temple

**Setting:** Drowned City, a month into Corvin's ride south from Ashvale — the first time he's crossed back into the old Empire's own ground since the truce. The painting on his wall at home called it fire from bank to bank; what's actually there is gulls, drowned towertops still showing their old colour under a century of salt, a heron standing where a wall used to be, and a salt breeze instead of smoke.

Corvin led his horse down to the water's edge and stood there a long while, the cloth's weight in one coat pocket and the ring's weight on his hand, and said out loud to no one in particular: "I don't know which one of you I actually came for." The gulls didn't answer. That much, at least, matched the painting.

A head appeared on a ruined rooftop. "Who goes there?" a man's voice called down.

Corvin didn't reach for anything at his belt. "Corvin Varn," he called back, plain as he'd have given it at any table in Ashvale. "Not here to take anything that's yours. Didn't think anyone was left to ask." He added the rest anyway, since half-answers never sat right with him. "Came to see the ground my grandmother died on. Didn't expect company."

The man gasped. "Varn?" A second figure climbed onto the rooftop beside him — a stance Corvin recognized even from the water below. Lilianne of the Woods.

He went still in a way that had nothing to do with caution. He'd never stood in a room with her — only ever felt her from across a board, a banner pried loose twice, warbands burned to nothing while he watched. The woman climbing down didn't carry herself like the Chancellor who'd done any of it; something in the stance had already changed since the last report he'd read of her.

"Lilianne." No title added, same as he'd have said any name he was still deciding how to hold. He didn't move to meet her halfway, but didn't step back either. "Didn't expect the Chancellor of Ashvale's own truce out here of all places." Then the honest question underneath it, since dressing things up had never been his habit. "Your father's hand ended what my grandmother started here. I came to stand where that happened. I'm asking plainly, not looking for a fight over it — what brought you?"

It was hard to tell her expression from the distance. "I... see," she said, after a silence. "Of course, Sir Varn, you are welcome to stay as long as you like. Won't you join us so that we may speak?"

Corvin inclined his head — not quite a bow, more the nod he'd give Perrin across a mustering table. "Sir Varn's more title than anyone's given me since the banners first went up," he said, dry, "but I'll take the seat over the argument." He looped the horse's reins to a half-drowned post and picked his way up the same unhurried way he'd crossed the Temple's broken stone a month back — checking his footing, not his welcome. "I'm not here to relitigate anything that happened over a war-table, Lilianne. Whatever this is, I'd rather hear it than guess at it."

Lilianne smiled. "The war-table. The banners going up. It's always struck me as odd that we reach for such tidy words to describe something this messy. It seems hardly appropriate, does it?" She gestured for him to sit — the man had left. "I'm here for two days, Sir Varn. Investigating a private matter. It seems fate has a flair for the dramatic." She poured tea, then sat. "How is it... for you? Being here, I mean."

Corvin took the tea more out of habit than thirst. "The tidiness never sat right with me either," he admitted. "The war-table. The banners going up. The prophecies the old women read into the smoke — words that let you hold a whole war in one hand and put it down when you're tired. Doesn't leave much room for a heron standing where a wall used to be." As for how it was: "Quieter than I expected. That's the honest half. I grew up on a painting of this place — fire bank to bank, her on her knees in it with a sword she wasn't putting down. I've carried that picture longer than I've carried her name. None of it matches what's actually here. No smoke. Just birds." He turned the ring once, an old habit he didn't try to hide. "I came ready to grieve something specific, and instead I'm sitting across from the daughter of the man who ended it, drinking tea neither of us poured for ourselves. Strange is the truest word I've got for it. Not bad. Just strange."

Lilianne's expression softened. "Yes... strange. When first I came here, I'd just learned there was to be war. I believed wholeheartedly I could resolve the situation peacefully. It seems I have failed even more miserably than my father has."

Corvin didn't rush to talk her out of it. "I heard what happened at Buried Giant," he said, evenly, no accusation in it. "Sorrel told it to me herself, the honest version. So I won't tell you it wasn't a failure. It was." He let that sit. "But I've rebuilt from nothing more times this war than I can count on one hand. Failure's not the same as finished — it's just where the count resets." He held her eyes. "Your father spent years hunting ghosts instead of ever resetting anything. You buried the fire instead, and swore something new over it. That's not nothing, whatever it cost you getting there."

"Yes... I swore things will be different now. I've buried the flame." She looked up at him. "My half, at least. Would you tell me, Sir Varn... why recruit the Fire Talkers to your cause? Why did you try to raise your rebellion with them of all people?"

Corvin turned the question over before answering it. "Because turning her away would've been easier, and easy's not the same as honest. Her clan burned back two of my grandmother's charges before your father finished the third. I could've heard that and sent her home, called it grief, called it respect for the dead. It would've been neither — it would've been me handing the debt off to someone else to carry instead of holding it myself." He glanced at the water. "So I kept her at my table instead. We never called it forgiven. We agreed to sit with it side by side rather than pretend a season had settled anything." Dry: "I won't lie and say her suit didn't help me raise the coin I needed. But that's not why I kept her. I kept her because she was willing to sit facing the door with me and not flinch from what either of us was owed. Turning her away would've meant I flinched first."

She smiled slightly. "Eloquently put." Her smile vanished. "Would you tell me, Sir Varn... how is she? How is Sorrel?"

Corvin weighed how much of it was his to tell. "Finding her feet. Perrin and I have both offered her ground in Ashvale — she took the night to decide before answering either one, which tells you more about her than anything I could say plain. She's not broken, if that's what you're asking. She left something with me at the Temple she'd carried a long while, and walked away lighter than she'd been carrying it." He paused, weighing whether the rest was kind or just honest, and gave her the honest one. "I won't pretend I know what's actually between the two of you. But I'll give it to you plain: last I heard her say your name, there wasn't much hope left in it. She said there was nothing of Lily left under the Chancellor's mantle worth waiting on. I don't know if that's still true. I only know it was, when she said it." Quieter: "Might be worth finding out yourself, now that the flame's in the ground instead of in your hand."

Lilianne exhaled slowly. "I see. Yes. I see, that is good, I suppose. That's for the best." She looked at him. "Can I help you with what you seek here, Sir Varn?"

Corvin considered the offer before taking it. "Maybe. I didn't come looking for proof of anything — I don't think there's a version of this where someone hands me a clean answer and I walk home certain. But there's a piece I don't have that you might." He explained: Sorrel's letter, Isolde's confession to Wren Sable the last day of her life, a debt neither Sorrel nor Wren ever decided the shape of. "You mentioned a letter — someone claiming to have survived the battle here, the one that sent your father hunting ghosts for a year before he vanished. If that person's still findable, they might be the only living soul who actually heard what she said. I'm not asking you to hand me your father's grief along with mine. I'm asking if you know where that letter came from."

She looked at him appraisingly. "I don't know anything more about the letter," she said. "Wherever my father is, what he's doing is a mystery. I cannot promise that it's related. But my father did tell stories of his friendship with Wren when Wren was still alive. Apparently, your grandmother had him take the blame for a crime she committed, and he lived as a criminal for years before the war started. I'm afraid I can't tell more — until my father returns, the living history book. It's one of the few advantages of having a father who doesn't age," she added with a shrug.

Corvin sat with that a moment, turning the ring the way he did when something needed to actually settle. "That matches better than the painting ever did," he said finally, flat. "The loyalists always told it as three broken charges and a woman who wouldn't kneel. The exiles told it as a confession, too late to help anyone. Sounds like both sides were half right and neither one knew the other half." He looked at the water. "She let a man live as a criminal for years so she wouldn't have to be one. Then she waited until she was dying to say so." He didn't dress that up as anything gentler. "I don't get to be angry on behalf of a man I never met, at a woman I never met either — that's not mine to spend. But I know which half of the debt just got heavier, and it isn't the half the Crown's still telling stories about." He glanced back to her. "Thank you for telling me plain instead of comfortable. That's twice today." Dry, despite himself: "A father who doesn't age. That's one more thing I didn't expect to hear before breakfast."

She let out a dry chuckle. "You hadn't heard? The Sorcerer of the Woods. The big mysterious ageless druid who talks to trees and makes people kiss out their worries with a smile. I doubt my title as the Bandit Queen will have as much an impact — then again, he didn't have a giant python for a pet. That's something."

Corvin allowed something in the family of a laugh. "I heard about the python secondhand — Perrin's still sour about it, something about even dice totals and a fight he had to size twice because of it." He shook his head. "Bandit Queen. Better ring to it than 'Exile who kept losing the same valley twice,' which is what they called me for a while." He drank the tea, finally. "Trees and kissed-away worries versus a python that eats rivals whole. I think you got the better end of that legacy, honestly. Yours at least keeps people honest."

"Now now, I hear you're doing quite well these days," she smirked. "If rumours are to be believed, I shall be calling you Sir Ashe before long?"

Corvin nearly choked on the tea, setting the cup down with more care than it needed. "Rumors travel faster than Fenn does, apparently." Plainer, the amusement not quite hiding the truth under it: "Ashvale suits him. Ashe suits him too, and he earned every letter of it his own way. But I'll keep Varn, thank you — I only just finished deciding what I actually owe the name. Seems early to hand it back before I've paid it down." The corner of his mouth moved, finally. "Though I haven't told him that in so many words yet. You may have just made my next conversation with him more interesting than I planned."

"Excellent! Why, you may find yourself with a second ring to idly play with."

Corvin turned the ring once, on cue, entirely without meaning to — which only proved her point. "If it happens, you'll hear about it the same way you heard about the first thing — through Fenn, three days late, with the details wrong." He shook his head, but the almost-smile stayed. "I came here to bury one debt in daylight, Lilianne, not open a second one before I've finished the first. Ask me again in a year."

"I will." She rose. "I must say, I had not expected to find you such an agreeable conversationalist."

Corvin rose as well. "Nor I you, if I'm honest — though I'll admit the tea helped." He extended a hand, plain, no ceremony. "Thank you for this. Both halves of it — the hard part and the easy part." Quieter: "For what it's worth, I don't think your father's the only one who gets to decide what this place means anymore. Might be worth remembering that, whatever else you find out here these two days."

"Thank you," she said, a full smile on her face. "And let me give you some parting wisdom as well: your grandmother's choices are not yours, Sir Varn. Please live your own life. Don't live the rest of your life with a debt that was made long before you were born."

Corvin held her hand a moment longer before letting go, weighing the advice the way he weighed everything else — carefully, not instantly. "That's the second time in a season someone's told me her debt isn't mine to finish. Sorrel said something close to it, in a letter. I didn't listen well the first time either." He let a real smile through, not the dry one. "I won't promise you I'll manage it. Old habits don't unlearn themselves just because two women I respect both told me the same true thing inside a month. But I heard you. That's more than I ever gave the painting." He gathered his coat, the ring and the cloth both settled where he'd put them, and looked out at the water once more before turning to go. "Safe travels, Lilianne. I hope you find your own answer here too, whatever it turns out to be."

"Be well, Sir Varn."
