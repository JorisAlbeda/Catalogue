# Sorrel Wick, Perrin Ashe, and Corvin Varn — the Ruined Temple, two months after the war

In two months, a lot has changed. The Empire has declared a truce following Perrin's surrender, and allowed him to keep the lands he conquered. He has unified them under a new name - Ashvale. And so it is that when Sorrel and her companions arrive at Fertile Valley, they enter a brand new capital, with activity and the enticing energy of new hope, under the leadership of Perrin, Warden of Ashvale. From rumours, she hears that he and Corvin have been spending an awful lot of time together, and within a few days, she has arranged a meeting with both of them at a unique location - the Ruined Temple, the last remains of the empire Perrin's grandfather once ruled.

Sorrel had picked the spot herself, not either of theirs, and she got there early enough to walk the broken foot of the temple wall twice before anyone arrived — enough stone left standing to tell you it had mattered once, not enough to tell you what it had actually been for. She was still crouched by a fallen lintel, tracing a worn-off carving with one ink-stained finger, when she heard the two of them approaching together, already mid-conversation, already comfortable in each other's silences the way people get when they've spent a season deciding whether to trust each other and come out the other side having decided yes.

She didn't stand right away. "Warden," she said first, to Perrin, without looking up yet — testing the word in her own mouth before she used it to his face. "That's going to take some getting used to. I remember when you were just the man who kept losing this exact valley to Corvin, back before either of you had a name worth keeping."

She rose then, dusting the old stone off her hands, and looked between them both properly for the first time — the ease between them, the matching quiet, the way neither one had bothered correcting her use of a title that clearly still fit them both differently. "I picked this ground on purpose," she said. "Your grandfather's, and unless I've heard wrong, still the one place in Ashvale nobody's actually put a name to yet — nobody's rule, nobody's claim, just old stone that remembers more than either of us do. Seemed like the fairest place to ask two men who've apparently gotten very close, very fast, exactly what it is I'm walking into here — and whether there's room in it for someone who isn't looking to rule anything anymore, just to stop being homeless."
Corvin didn't look to Perrin to answer for him — he'd learned these past two months that the two of them thinking as one wasn't the same as speaking as one. He crossed the last of the broken stone instead, close enough that answering quietly would still carry. "You're not fishing for that half, Wick. You're too plain a woman to dress up a question you already know the shape of. So I'll answer the one you actually asked." A glance back at Perrin — not for permission, just habit by now — before he finished. "There's room. I spent every stretch of that whole war losing everything I'd built and starting again from nothing — you watched me do it twice, closer than most people would've let themselves watch. I'm not going to stand on ground I only half-earned and tell a woman who did the same thing I did, better, that there isn't space in it for her." He nodded once toward Perrin. "Ashvale's his to grant, not mine. But whatever I've got a say in, you've got it too. Homeless was never a debt I let sit on anyone I actually respected. You're not sitting on it either."

Perrin hadn't said anything yet, and Sorrel had noticed — he could tell by the way she'd left the last of the silence open for him on purpose, same as she used to leave a message unanswered until she was good and ready. He picked his way over the same broken stone Corvin had crossed, close enough to the old carving to see what her finger had been tracing — a name, or what was left of one, worn past reading by the time either of them were born. "Warden's going to take me some getting used to too," he said. "Wasn't sure I'd earned the right to call myself anything at all, some mornings that war." He looked up from the stone to her, plain. "I sent you three messages that whole campaign asking you to do something you never did, and you never once wrote back to tell me no. I used to think that meant you weren't listening. Took me till after it ended to work out you probably heard every word and just didn't owe me an answer for free." A beat, the closest he came to a smile all day. "You don't owe me one now either. Nobody's asking what you'll do with a home before you've had one long enough to want something out of it. Pick your ground — Narrow Pass already knows your name better than it knows mine, seeing as it's the last place you stood before all this changed — or don't pick at all yet. Either way you're not walking back out of this valley without a roof somewhere in it that's yours."

Sorrel let that sit a moment before she trusted her own voice with it. "I came here to ask a question, not to be handed the answer before I'd finished asking it," she said, quieter than either of them had heard her all day. She looked down at the worn-away name under her own finger, then back up. "Give me till morning before I pick. I've made every claim I've ever held on the same afternoon I first wanted it, and look how each of those ended." A pause, almost dry again. "One night of thinking it over first might be the only thing about me worth changing."

Corvin allowed himself the almost-smile she'd have called him out for. "Take the night, then. I've learned that much myself — the ground's still there in the morning, whichever way you decide." He picked his way back off the broken stone without pressing further, the debt of the day handed over rather than collected on.

Before he could leave, she reached into her coat and unpinned the scrap of grey-green cloth she'd carried since before either of them had known her — worn soft at the folds from years of not being looked at. "Before morning, though, there's one thing I'd rather not carry into a new claim without asking first." She held it out to Corvin, not quite offering it, just showing it. "This was hers. Isolde's. Wren Sable left it to me along with everything else he never explained, and I've worn it longer than I've worn anything of my own without once knowing if I had the right to. I know how she died now — Drowned City, three charges, your own people's fire turning back the first two before the Sorcerer himself finished what the third one started. What I don't know is whether anyone ever actually told her she was owed better than that. You'd know better than I would whether that debt got paid, or whether it's still sitting where she left it."

Corvin took the cloth carefully, both hands, the way he'd take anything else long uncollected. "Nobody ever told me either, if that helps — I've spent this whole war not knowing which half of her to answer to." He folded it once, the same crease it already had, and set it against the ring's own chain rather than pocketing it loose. "It's not paid. But it's not sitting unclaimed anymore, either."

Sorrel watched him do it and didn't reach to stop him. "That's a fairer answer than I came here expecting," she said. "I wasn't looking to hand you her debt along with mine — just to stop being the only one who remembered it was owed." She let her hand fall back to her side, lighter than it had been in longer than a season. "Keep it better than Wren Sable did. He never told anyone what it cost him to carry it either."

Corvin didn't try pricing what that cost Wren Sable — not his debt to weigh. "I'll try," was all he gave her back, plain as everything else he'd said that day, the closest thing to a promise he'd made since the cloth changed hands. He said nothing more, and looked toward Perrin instead, done with his own part of it for now.

Perrin had stayed quiet through that part on purpose — some debts weren't his to referee, and that one clearly wasn't his. He was the one who moved first once it closed, mostly so neither of them would have to be. "Ruined Temple doesn't have a roof worth sleeping under, whatever else it's still good for," he said, nodding back toward the valley proper. "Mill's got a spare room Bettina's kept made up longer than she's ever said why. Take the night there, Wick — decide in the morning, same as you said. Nobody's counting the hours against you."

"Bettina's room, then." Sorrel pinned nothing back inside her coat where the cloth had been — left the space empty on purpose, the first night in longer than she could count that it wasn't there. "I've said everything I came here to say. The rest can wait for morning, same as the ground."
