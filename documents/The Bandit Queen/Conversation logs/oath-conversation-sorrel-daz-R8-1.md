# Conversation — Sorrel Wick, her advisers, and Daz (Round 8)

Narrow Pass narrowed to nothing wider than a cart track by the time they reached Daz's door — a low stone hut wedged into the defile's own throat, smoke curling from a chimney too crooked to have been built by anyone who cared what it looked like. Sorrel knocked once, out of habit rather than hope anyone would mind, and let herself in when nobody answered.

Daz was exactly where gossip said she'd be: cross-legged by the fire, working a knot out of a length of red cord with her teeth, older than the stories made her sound and twice as unbothered. "Well," she said, not looking up. "The Chancellor's charity case. Sit if you're staying — the floor doesn't care whose banner you used to carry."

"Used to," Sorrel said, and sat.

Ossa took up the doorway instead, arms crossed, the posture of a man who trusted a room better with an exit at his back. "You've got some nerve, hosting exiles in a Bandit's own hall while the woman who owns your old contract is off winning a war."

"She doesn't own anything of mine anymore," Daz said, still working the knot. "Perrin's boys hold this ground now, not her Rangers. I served whoever paid on time, and Lilianne's coin got slower every season after her father's box went in the ground — you learn to read a patron the way you read weather. I read hers early. Looks like your girl read it late." She glanced up at Sorrel then, not unkindly. "No offense."

"None taken," Sorrel said. "I read it exactly on time. I just kept betting the weather would hold anyway."

Fen Quill had drifted to the far wall on the pretense of warming his hands, close enough to a shelf of small clay jars that his fingers had already found the nearest one and turned it over twice before setting it back exactly as he'd found it — old habit, no real intention behind it tonight, just checking the shape of a room the way some men check a door is locked. "For what it's worth," he said, "she didn't win a war. She won a fight. There's a difference, and I don't think she's noticed it yet."

"Sounds like something a man says right before he loses the second fight too," Daz said.

"It's something I say because I've watched Sorrel lose exactly twice now and come out of both still owed something," Fen Quill said. "I'm not in the business of noble defeats. I'm in the business of what's left over after them. There's usually more than people think."

Marlow Ash hadn't moved from just inside the door, watching the fire instead of the conversation, the way he watched everything — sideways, patient, like the real information was always in what nobody meant to show him. "What's left over," he said, half to himself. "That's the only part of this I ever trusted anyway. Nobody plans what gets thrown away. That's why it tells the truth and the rest doesn't." He looked at Sorrel directly for the first time since they'd arrived. "She burned every one that lost. Not most. Every one. That's not a woman clearing a battlefield. That's a woman making sure there's nothing left to read afterward — no leavings, nothing for someone like me to go through and learn something true from. I've spent this whole war finding out what people are by what they throw away. Lilianne just told me, as loud as she's ever told anyone anything, that she doesn't want to be found out that way. Not by you. Maybe not by anyone."

Sorrel was quiet a moment, turning that over. "She used to leave things lying around on purpose," she said. "Half a sentence, a door left open a crack. I used to think that was carelessness. I think now it might have been the only honest thing she still let herself do." She looked at the fire instead of any of them. "There isn't a version of tonight where I pretend that's still true. She didn't leave me anything this time. Not a look, not a word, not even the courtesy of a real reason beyond the obvious one. Just the fire, and then the road."

"Good," Ossa said, from the doorway, flat as ever. "Took you long enough to stop waiting for one."

"That's not comfort, Ossa."

"Wasn't meant to be. You've got what's left of the people who followed you out of Buried Giant, Fen Quill and Marlow and me still at your elbow, and no crown, no banner, no roof — which, near as I can tell, is exactly what you started this whole business with, except now nobody's confused about what you actually are. You spent five rounds trying to be somebody's Citizen and one trying to be somebody's Successor and you lost both. Try being an Exile properly for once. See how that one fits."

Daz snorted, finally setting the cord down whole. "He's not wrong. I've watched three regimes come through this pass in my lifetime, and the ones who lasted weren't the ones who kept trying to get back what got taken. They built somewhere the last war hadn't already decided the shape of." She nodded toward the door, the dark defile beyond it, the whole unclaimed weight of the Hinterland past it. "There's more world out there than the two valleys you two used to fight over as girls. Might be time you noticed that, now that she's made it so plain you're not welcome back."

Sorrel didn't answer right away. She sat with the fire, the cord in Daz's hands, Fen Quill's stolen glance at the jar, Marlow's quiet grief for a discard pile that hadn't been left standing, Ossa's blunt refusal to let her mourn it prettier than it deserved — and found, underneath all of it, something that wasn't quite hope and wasn't quite grief either. Just arithmetic, the kind she trusted. The followers who'd come this far with her. Fen Quill, Marlow Ash, Ossa, none of them gone. No ledger to keep for anyone but herself, for the first time since she'd taken the desk at Charming Valley.

"All right," she said, to the fire more than any of them. "Properly, then."

Nobody answered that. Nobody needed to.

Daz was quiet a while after that, working the cord back into a knot instead of out of one — an old habit, Sorrel realized, something her hands did when her head was somewhere else. "I've got maybe a dozen of my own left who still call me chief more out of memory than pay," she said eventually. "Perrin's boys tolerate them as neighbors, not soldiers, which isn't the same as a future. I could keep sitting here reading weather I've already read." She set the cord down for good this time. "Or I could send a few of them out the door with someone who still hasn't figured out where she's walking. Two, maybe three. Enough to make it worth remembering, not enough to leave nothing at this fire."

Sorrel looked up. "You don't owe me that."

"No," Daz agreed. "But nobody's asked what I'm owed in longer than I care to admit, and you walked in here still doing sums instead of asking to be pitied. That's worth something in a Bandit's ledger, if not the Chancellor's." She whistled, short and low, toward the back of the hut, and two shapes stirred from where they'd been dozing near the store-room — younger than Daz by decades, unbothered by being volunteered. "Go with her. Don't come back with a good excuse if it goes wrong."

Sorrel didn't smile, exactly. But something in her shoulders let go that hadn't let go all night. "I'll take that as an introduction, not a favor," she said. "I keep those separate."

"So I noticed," said Daz.
