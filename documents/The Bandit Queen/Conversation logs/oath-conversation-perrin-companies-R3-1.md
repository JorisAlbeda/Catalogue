# Perrin Ashe — the rally at Fertile Valley (Round 3)

Perrin Ashe stood at the fork where the river cut white through Fertile Valley, and let the whole gathered company see him do nothing for a long moment.

They'd come at his word — companies drawn up along both banks, spears grounded, tired from two seasons of walking behind him without knowing why. Word had gone out from the mill three days before: *come to the valley, all of you, and bring what you can spare.* Bettina Marsh stood at the edge of it now with her arms folded and Halvard's old iron key still hanging where it always hung, watching him the way she'd watched him since he was a boy who broke things and called it an accident.

He'd carried the charter for two years without saying its name aloud — the one that had wandered out of this valley's own keeping and turned up filed among a usurper's papers, like the place itself had been quietly erased from the record of who it belonged to. He touched the scar over his eyebrow, the one he still wouldn't explain, and thought of his grandfather standing on ground not far from this exact fork, sixty years gone, swearing to a crowd half this size that he'd hold what was his by right and let the rest of the world argue about it after.

"My grandfather stood a mile from here," Perrin said, and didn't raise his voice, so that the ones at the back had to lean in to catch it — which was its own kind of quiet the crowd hadn't expected from him. "He swore an oath the day they made him Chancellor, and the world broke that oath for him before he'd finished swearing it. I used to think that was the end of the story. I don't think that anymore."

He drew the folded charter from inside his cloak — sun-faded, the wax long since cracked away — and didn't read from it. He didn't need to.

"I'm not asking you to hold a mill, or a mine, or a stretch of river. I'm asking you to help me take back what was ours before either of us was born, one field at a time, until nobody left standing can call it borrowed anymore. That's not a small thing to swear to. I'm not swearing it small."

He let the charter fall shut in his hand.

"This is Halvard Cray's war. It's mine now. If any of you would rather go home, go now — I'll think no less of you for it. The rest of you, get some rest. We start at first light."

Nobody left. Bettina didn't move from the mill door, but something in the set of her shoulders eased, as if she'd been waiting a long time to hear someone say it plainly. The companies broke into their fires as the sun went down over water that had, for one evening at least, stopped belonging to anyone else.
