# Conversation — Sorrel Wick & Lilianne of the Woods (Round 5)

Sorrel didn't jump at the scuffling behind the far tapestry, didn't reach for anything — just went very still, then looked between the two shapes stepping out of the dark, more tired than caught. Lilianne first, and behind her, unmistakably, Naiad.

"How much of that did you get," Sorrel said, "or should I just start from the part where I said it plainly."

Naiad draws a shortsword, stepping in front of her daughter. For a moment Lilianne's eyes are filled with hurt — then it's gone, replaced by something colder and more regal, a Chancellor scorned. "Explain yourself," she says, holding Sorrel's gaze.

"Put that away, Naiad. I haven't drawn on anyone in this room and I'm not about to start." Sorrel didn't move, didn't reach for anything of her own — kept her hands loose at her sides where both of them could see them. "I never lied to you, Lily. I hold that banner because if this Empire falls — to Perrin, to a bad roll of the dice, to anything neither of us actually controls — I want to be the one standing in what's left of it, not whoever gets there first. That's not the same as wanting it to fall. But I won't stand here and tell you I haven't been getting ready for the day it might, just because you heard me say it plainly to the only three people I ever say plain things to."

"In other words, you had the dagger ready, just in case I was too weak? You make a mockery of me, Sorrel."

"It's not a mockery, and it was never about you being weak." Sorrel let that land before she went on. "I've kept that desk long enough to learn that keeping the peace and being safe are two different promises, and only one of them's ever actually made to people like me. Your crown was never guaranteed — not the day your father died, not the day you picked it up after him. I'd have held this same banner if you were the surest ruler this land's ever seen, because I don't put my faith in anyone being strong enough forever. Not you, not myself. Ask anyone who's ever tried to get an unearned favor out of my desk whether that's new."

"I told you, my father is not dead!" Lilianne shouts, the words ringing through the halls. Naiad murmurs, "Your Excellency," and Lilianne draws a deep breath. "I've heard enough." A moment of silence rings louder than her shout had. Then: "Sorrel Wick, you are hereby banished from the Empire. You will live the rest of your days as an Exile, and be forgotten. You and your followers have three days to leave Buried Giant."

Sorrel didn't answer right away. When she did, her voice came out level, almost arithmetic — the tone of someone doing sums rather than pleading. "Three days." A beat. "You'll owe me favor before I go, then — five of it, less what my own banner buys me off the price. Naiad can check the ledger herself if she doesn't trust my count." She let that sit, cruel in its plainness, before something more honest cracked through underneath it. "I meant what I told you at Drowned City. About coming back for you, not against you. I still mean it. You're just going to have to watch me keep that promise from outside the walls now instead of in."

Naiad's lip curled. "You overestimate her Excellency's mercy once again. The People's Favor was recovered this morning — from Corvin." A beat, savoring it. "Don't overstay your welcome. Lest my Rangers chase you out." Lilianne looked at Sorrel, mouth open on something that never came, then turned and led her mother from the room.

Sorrel didn't call after them. She stood alone a long moment in the low candlelight, the shape of everything actually lost only arriving now, all at once — not the banner alone, not even the title, but the particular way Lilianne hadn't managed the last word. When she finally moved, it wasn't toward the door. "Well," she said, to the dark where the other three still waited, and nothing else came for a while.
