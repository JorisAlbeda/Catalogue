# Conversation — Sorrel Wick & Lilianne of the Woods (Round 4)

Sorrel climbed the tower stair Lilianne had claimed for herself, boots loud on stone still damp from sixty years under water, and found her exactly where the note implied she'd be — no guards posted between them, which was either trust or carelessness. "You could've just asked me yourself," she said, "instead of sending a man with a scroll to do it for you."

Lilianne is seated at a desk, maps and reports strewn across it, her purple mantle discarded in the corner. She drops her pen as Sorrel barges in, reaching for a knife — then softens, seeing her childhood friend. "I'm your ruler now, you know," she says dryly. "And a Bandit Queen, apparently. The least you can do is knock."

"A queen who still flinches at a closed door's worth noting," Sorrel said, not moving from the threshold. "Knock's easy once you start locking that door like you mean it."

She grins. "It's good to see you again, Sorrel. Come in and close that door before more of my subjects come begging for an audience."

Sorrel stepped inside and pulled the door shut behind her, the latch catching louder than she meant it to. "Good to see you too, Lily — though of every ruin in the Provinces to make your camp, you had to pick the one everyone still tells stories about your father in."

Lilianne bites her lip, eyes drifting to the bed in the corner. "I know," she says eventually, blinking. "It's silly, isn't it? This shouldn't be about him. He's gone, and he left me an empire to run. I told myself I'd come here to find answers, and now I'm just obsessing over how this old bed smells like my dad."

Sorrel didn't sit, didn't reach for her, just looked at the bed like it deserved the same plain treatment as anything else in the room. "It's not silly. I've got a scrap of cloth sewn inside my own coat I still haven't told you the story of — you don't get to decide when a thing stops being about someone."

Lilianne exhales, then looks at the cloth curiously. "What's that?"

Sorrel touched the coat's inside seam without pulling the cloth out. "Wren gave it to me the same day as that ledger box marked SANCTUARY — said it belonged to Isolde Varn, and that whether she ever deserved her own apology wasn't mine to decide, only mine to keep asking. Never told me why it had to be me holding it instead of her."

"Isolde Varn..." Lily frowns. "Is that why you came here? To visit her grave?"

"No — I came because this ruin still hands out a favor or a secret to whoever's fool enough to stand in it, same as it did last time." Sorrel's eyes went to the water lapping at the tower's base. "Didn't put the rest together until just now, standing here. This is where she died, isn't it. Your father's own hand, if the story I was told is right."

"Come here." She walks to the largest window, pointing at a shape barely visible in the dusk — hard to tell if it's a statue or an altar, but it looks like some kind of memorial. "That's where she fell. That's where the Fire Talkers took her." She eyes her friend. "It was always a fairy tale. And now I'm standing here — and now Perrin's declared war on us. Everything's becoming so real, Sorrel."

Sorrel didn't offer her anything soft, just looked at the shape in the dusk a moment longer. "Real's better than the fairy tale you grew up on — you can actually do something about real. And you're not standing here alone with it. I put my own people on Charming Valley's walls this week for exactly the reason you think."

"Yeah..." She nods slowly. "Yes. You're right. You can actually do something about real. Let's do something real." She turns away from the window. "Thanks for defending the capital. Say... what's all this I'm hearing about you raising the Banner of People's Favor? Mom's been warning me about you starting a rebellion."

"It's not a rebellion, it's the opposite." Sorrel held her gaze, not backing off it. "A Citizen who lets the people's favor sit forgotten in somebody's ledger isn't doing her job — I'd rather this Empire fall with me holding what's actually theirs than watch it happen from the sidelines. Your mother's welcome to worry about me. I'd worry more about what she's still not telling either of us about this place."

Lilianne studies her face for a long time, not responding.

Sorrel let her look, in no hurry to fill the silence with anything she'd already said. "I came back here for you, Lily. Not against you. Figured that was worth saying once, plainly, before I go." She didn't wait for an answer before letting herself back out the door.

"Sorrel, wait, I didn't mean—" The door closes before she finishes. Lilianne makes a move toward the bed, then stops herself. She gathers her composure, locks the door behind Sorrel, and returns to the desk, picking her pen back up.
