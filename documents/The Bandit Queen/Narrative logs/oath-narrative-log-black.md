# Narrative Log — Black

## Round 1

**Development:** Arrived at Fertile Valley to find it already seized by a fellow Exile, Corvin Varn — ground Perrin's own Motivation and Flaw both pull him toward holding, whether or not that's wise.

**Discussion:** Fenn backed splitting the men before the fight, and the price paid to win it; his warning that the field would stand empty behind them finally landed only when fresh hands came up from the lake, and Perrin kept one home instead of marching every last one out.

**Action:** Perrin broke Corvin's last man at the cost of one of his own and stood alone on Fertile Valley's ground. A second omen surfaced while he turned the valley over looking for answers; he kept it close and said nothing of what it promised, tucking it in with the company he already trusted. He called fresh hands up from the lake to hold what he'd taken, then made camp — the mill and Bettina's key still unvisited.

## Round 2 — Converse with Corvin

Called Corvin to the mill, not to refight what was already settled twice over. Traded the obvious question — why he keeps taking this ground — for the real one: what Corvin knew about the key Bettina keeps, and the charter it used to open. Got no answer on the charter, but got something better: Corvin's willing to wait and be paid back in that answer instead of blood. Full exchange: `Game/Story/Conversation logs/oath-conversation-perrin-corvin-R2-1.md`.

## Round 2

**Development:** Perrin still holds just the one stretch of ground, tied with a stranger he's never met for who holds the most of it — the mill's question can wait; this one's simpler to answer.

**Discussion:** Fenn called it a coin flip with only one man behind it, and said plainly the tie mattered to Perrin more than it probably should; Perrin heard him out and went anyway — an empty claim still beats none.

**Action:** He left Fertile Valley to its own three and crossed the Hinterland to the ground Corvin had already walked through and left bare, putting his last free man against whatever still haunts it.

## Round 3

**Development:** Both halves of the hidden promise are true at once for the first time — Perrin holds more ground than anyone at this table, and enough omens have surfaced elsewhere to matter — but it isn't his to claim until his own morning comes around again, three others' turns from now.

**Discussion:** Fenn wanted the men called up before the card went face-up, not after — no sense handing a rival warning before the ground under it was actually thick enough to hold. Perrin did both, just in that order.

**Action:** He gathered fresh hands at the mine and thickened the line there, crossed home and did the same at the valley, and only then turned the hidden card face up for anyone at the table to read. The mill went unvisited again.

## Round 3 — The rally at Fertile Valley

Called every company he had back to the fork in the river and said the charter's name aloud for the first time, standing where his grandfather once swore the oath the world broke on him. Bettina watched from the mill door and didn't leave. Full address: `Game/Story/Conversation logs/oath-conversation-perrin-companies-R3-1.md`.

## Round 4

**Development:** Word of a stolen crown letting the Empire claim bandit ground without a fight reached Perrin the same way most useful things do — secondhand, and irritating for how obvious it made the alternative. Narrow Pass sits held by nobody but bandits, the same kind he's already broken twice.

**Discussion:** Fenn backed the fight itself without hesitation, but called the split of men wrong-headed — thinning the valley to two while the mine sat fat and untouched was guarding the wrong door. Perrin took the correction and left three at home instead.

**Action:** He pulled two companies from the valley's own line, marched them out past ground he'd never before dared leave, and stood at the mouth of Narrow Pass with the bandits' own fire still burning in front of him. Ressa Kindler had found him at the mill before he left, facing the door the whole time she spoke — Corvin's debt over the key still stood, war or no war, and her parting word stuck harder than the reminder: a chancellor with five stolen treasures and six conquered holds would give him a better war than any impoverished Exile could. He went to the Pass anyway. The fight hadn't started yet when the light went out on the day's reckoning.

Two blades found their mark before a bandit ever lifted a shield, and one of his own men went down all the same — steel first, then something worse. Green fire answered from behind the bandits' own line, sworn to ground Perrin had thought was only ever guarded by rabble; Ressa's warning made ugly, immediate sense. He let the last man stand down rather than throw him after a fight already lost twice over. Narrow Pass held its bandits. Perrin turned back toward the valley two companies lighter, and understood for the first time exactly what kind of war he'd actually declared.

Their chief came out to meet him anyway, not an hour after trying to burn him alive — Daz, broad and unbothered, willing to do business with the very man who'd just tried to take his ground. Perrin traded him word of a caravan route for two sacks of grain and got a nod instead of an apology; there'd be no debt held over this one, only the next fight, whenever Perrin came back with enough men to actually win it.

## Round 5

**Development:** Perrin came back to Narrow Pass with no intention of fighting — just coin, and the chief who'd already shown he'd deal with anyone standing in front of him.

**Discussion:** Fenn granted that a thicker company doesn't answer the real question — why that ground burned at all — but didn't stand in the way of the caution itself, and liked keeping the new men loose rather than settled. He asked, plainly, for that other conversation before Perrin pointed anyone back at the Pass.

**Action:** Perrin bought two of Daz's own men off him with coin already in hand, kept them apart from the valley's own line, and marched them home. He stopped at the lake long enough to trade the last piece of information he'd been sitting on for another coin, banking it rather than spending it, and made camp with nothing more to show for the day than two loose companies and a fuller purse.

## Round 6 — A sparrow, answered

Word reached him of what the Chancellor's crown had just done to Corvin and to a woman he'd never met, exiled with a purse of coin for her trouble. Her own sparrow found him not long after, asking for a meeting at the Ruined Temple — his own ground, at least. He had Fenn seal a plain answer in plain wax: he'd come, but he wanted to hear from her own mouth why he shouldn't count himself next, and he wanted her arriving with fewer soldiers than the ones that had already burned two of his companies without her ever lifting a hand herself.

## Round 6 — The Chancellor's offer

She came as asked, riding in on something too large to call an escort, and offered him everything he'd actually wanted — the valley restored, a place at the Empire's own table, even his grandfather's own conqueror's mask handed back like a gift instead of a wound. He turned all of it down where he stood, in the one room that still remembered when this ground answered to his own name and no one else's. She didn't argue the point twice. She named him an enemy instead, plainly, and rode off before dawn broke all the way. Full exchange: `Game/Story/Conversation logs/oath-conversation-perrin-lilianne-R6-1.md`.

## Round 6 — Narrow Pass, for real this time

**Development:** An enemy now by her own word, Perrin went back to Narrow Pass a third time — not to test it, to end it. He'd learned by then that the bandits standing there had never really been alone.

**Discussion:** Fenn talked him down from six men to five, unwilling to leave the valley holding on a single pair of hands the same day the Chancellor had named him her enemy to his face. Perrin agreed, and left two behind instead of one.

**Action:** Five companies marched into ground that fought back harder than it ever had before — steel found less purchase than he'd hoped, and it took two good men sent forward to finally break the line. The three who walked away planted themselves on Narrow Pass and didn't look back toward home. For the first time since he'd crossed into the Hinterland, every stretch of it answered to Perrin Ashe alone.

Word reached him after, of a promise Corvin carried that would come due the moment his own next morning broke — and that Sorrel's own morning fell just before it. Perrin sent Fenn out one more time before resting, not for Sorrel's sake and not against Corvin's, only so that whatever got decided next got decided with everyone still standing still able to weigh in.

## Round 7 — Two more words sent

The Chancellor moved first after all — took the banner back from Corvin before either exile's own morning came around. Perrin had Fenn carry Sorrel the plain update, so she wouldn't spend anything chasing a fire already out. Then, for the first time, he sent word to Corvin himself, not through Ressa this time but plainly, his own hand behind it: the Chancellor had named them both her enemy in the same week, one way or another, and whatever came next, Perrin would rather spend what time was left fighting her than fighting a man he still owed an answer.

He sent Fenn up the same track a third time before the day was out, past courtesy this time and into plain asking: the whole Hinterland answered to him now and it still wasn't enough, and Sorrel was the one person left at the table who'd already proven she could take ground from the Chancellor when it suited her. He didn't dress it up as anything but what it was — his own gap to close, her choice to make it, nothing owed either way.

## Round 7 — A debt to an enemy

**Development:** Word came from Corvin's own man before the day was properly begun — Sorrel had found the last of the old claims to the throne, the one bound to the realm's darkest secret, and it named her the rightful ruler outright, the moment her own dawn came round again. Perrin's own morning falls last of anyone's, and hers next of all; there was no road left for him to close the gap himself, only one man left standing close enough in time to matter, and it wasn't him.

**Discussion:** Fenn didn't flinch at sending word to an enemy — argued it plainly, that a debt owed by a woman who'd already named him hers to hate still beat a table with no game left on it, Perrin's own included. Perrin agreed before Fenn had finished making the case.

**Action:** He sent Fenn to the Chancellor's own gate unescorted with the whole of it laid out plain, asking nothing back for the telling. Then he stopped short of making camp — if this was truly the last morning he'd get, sitting on it wasn't a plan. He pulled two companies off Narrow Pass's own line, left the third to mind the gate alone, and marched south into ground he'd never set foot on: Wastes, empty of any living ruler but claimed all the same by the same green fire that had cost him twice at the Pass. He didn't wait to be asked twice why he was fighting a fight that thin — the Hinterland alone had never been enough, and this was the last chance to make it matter before someone else's morning decided the whole table's fate for him.

Luck answered where caution hadn't: two blades found their mark and only one of his own men paid for it, the bandits' own green fire going out without so much as a scratch on Wastes' own soil. He planted his last man on the empty ground and turned to walk it — for the first time since he'd left Fertile Valley the very first week, more of the map answered to Perrin Ashe than to anyone else at the table.

## Round 8 — The key

**Development:** The math came back plain and unkind: Lilianne's own hoard of treasures and both banners together make her the Empire's rightful ruler regardless, no matter whose flag flies over the most ground when the reckoning comes — nothing left this day changes who wins. Colm found him again the same morning with Corvin's last, quietest ask yet: what did the key open, and what did he find.

**Discussion:** No war left to plan for, so there was nothing for Fenn to weigh in on this time — Perrin didn't ask, and for once didn't need to.

**Action:** He crossed back to Fertile Valley one final time and went to the mill, not to check that the key still hung there but to take it down. Bettina didn't stop him. She'd never once asked why he checked it every visit, and she didn't ask now — just watched him fit it to the small strongbox under the millstone that had sat locked since before either of them could say who'd locked it. It opened on the first try. Inside: no charter, no deed, nothing with the Chancellor's seal or anyone else's — just a single folded letter in a hand Perrin had never seen but somehow already knew, addressed to no one by name. Halvard Cray had written it to whoever came asking one day, the way Perrin himself had been asking without words for eight rounds running. It said, in fewer words than Perrin expected, that he'd spent his own reign mistaking the valley for the thing worth keeping, and that whoever read this should already know better than that by now, or should stop and learn it before the ground under them changed hands too. Perrin folded it once, the same crease Halvard had left in it, and put it back exactly where he'd found it. Some debts, once actually paid, don't need keeping. He sent Corvin the truth of it plain, the same way it had been asked.

## Round 8 — Corvin, at the mill again

Corvin came in person this time, no messenger between them. Didn't ask what Perrin meant to do with what men he had left once the reckoning decided things — said instead that he already knew what he was doing with what was left of the night. Perrin gave him the rest of what the ring had already been asking without words. Neither of them left before the lamp did. Full exchange: `Game/Story/Conversation logs/oath-conversation-corvin-perrin-R8-1.md`.

## Round 8 — Sorrel, at the Ruined Temple

Two months on, with the truce holding and Ashvale standing where an empire once claimed the ground, Sorrel found her way to Fertile Valley and picked the meeting place herself: the Ruined Temple, the one stretch of it nobody had put a name to yet. She wanted to know what she was walking into, arriving with nothing left to rule and no interest in ruling anything again. Perrin gave her the plain answer he'd owed her since the messages she'd never written back to — that silence was never refusal, and it still isn't owed one now — and offered her ground of her own to pick, no claim attached. Before she decided, she handed Corvin the cloth she'd carried unexplained since Wren Sable died and asked, plainly, whether Isolde Varn's debt was ever actually paid. Perrin closed the night by pointing her toward the mill's own spare room rather than push for an answer before morning. ## One year later

The mill wheel turns easier than it used to — Perrin had the sluice cleared out come spring, the first thing he'd actually built rather than taken. Bettina still keeps the key on its nail, but nobody checks it anymore; it hangs there because it's always hung there, not because either of them is still waiting for something to go wrong.

Harvest's in, the first one Ashvale's called its own rather than fought somebody else for, and half of Fertile Valley's turned out for it — trestle tables strung along the riverbank, Corvin arguing with a farmhand about grain weights he plainly doesn't understand, losing more often than not. He wears the ring on a plain cord around his neck these days instead of his finger; it never did fit right, and neither of them saw the point in forcing it. Perrin watches him lose an argument about turnips and doesn't step in.

A rider comes down from Narrow Pass midafternoon — one of Sorrel's, not Sorrel herself. She sends word more than she comes in person these days, running the pass her own way, answering to nobody's crown, least of all his. The message is short: the pass held fine through the thaw, Daz still charges Ashvale's own people double for anything they could've grown themselves, and she'll be down before the frost to finally collect on a promise about a spare room she never actually used.

Fenn finds him at the sluice gate near dusk, still counting wagon wheels under his breath out of a habit that has nothing left to be for. "Getting soft, Ashe," he says, watching Corvin lose another argument up the bank. Perrin doesn't argue the point. A year ago he'd have called it a flaw. Some mornings, he still isn't sure it isn't. Most mornings, he doesn't check.
