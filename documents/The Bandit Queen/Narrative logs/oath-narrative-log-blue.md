# Narrative Log — Blue

## Fertile Valley, the first days

Corvin reached Fertile Valley to find it squatted by bandits, with Perrin Ashe already camped on the very same ground — neither of them first, neither of them welcome. Hesper warned him against throwing every man he had into a first day's fight with nothing held in reserve, and later that giving up the traveling doctor's protection so soon after was a real loss, not a free hand — he overruled her both times. Ressa Kindler, newly arrived at his fire, only offered that three men hadn't been enough against bandits and wouldn't be enough against worse.

He fought the bandits off anyway and took Fertile Valley — and with it, Boiling Lake — at the cost of two men, then spent what was left of the day asking after anyone willing to follow him: kept Ressa Kindler close despite her clan's history with Isolde Varn, and folded a second, quieter promise into the ground he'd just bled for, telling no one yet what it actually asked of him.

## By the fire, with Ressa

Corvin asked her outright about the name he carries. Ressa told him plainly what her clan actually did at Drowned City sixty years ago — the fire that turned back two of Isolde Varn's charges, and the Sorcerer of the Woods's own hand on the third. Neither apologized. Neither left the fire. They settled on holding two debts neither of them chose, side by side, without pretending either one was settled.

## At the mill, with Perrin

Corvin came when Perrin called, more out of a dislike for unpaid questions than any real curiosity about the dirt between them. Learned nothing about the key or its charter — he'd never thought to ask what those walls used to guard while the ground was his — but left holding something else: Perrin's own word that he'd come back with an answer once that key had actually been used on something. Took the trade without complaint; collecting on a promise suited him better than holding ground ever did. Full exchange: `Game/Story/Conversation logs/oath-conversation-perrin-corvin-R2-1.md`.

## Leaving Fertile Valley behind

There was nothing left at Fertile Valley worth Corvin's time — no treasure worth the taking, no open promise at Boiling Lake, no debt still owed him there — and he'd already told Perrin plainly the ground didn't interest him anymore anyway. Hesper worried that walking off bled-for ground so soon looked like surrender dressed up as indifference; Ressa cared less for appearances than for the plain math of it — an empty field is only worth looking at, not holding, until men actually stand on it.

He left Fertile Valley behind for the last unclaimed stretch of the Hinterland, spending nearly everything he'd brought with him just to see what lay under it — a mine, guarding a cauldron he couldn't yet afford, and an empty stretch of ground beside it. He spent what little remained talking to whoever the mine and its village sent his way, and came home with a keeper for the old way station rather than the messenger or the mine-worker — someone who'd let him pass through free later, which mattered more to him than a single coin spent once. He made camp there with nothing left to spend.

## The cauldron's price

Someone else's men held the ground around him now, but the cauldron the mine had been sitting on all this time was still just sitting there, unclaimed by anyone with the coin to actually take it. Hesper allowed that a real prize in hand beats another debt owed, watching him hand over the one keepsake he'd carried since the day he first rode out, without complaint; Ressa's only worry was that it left him standing on someone else's ground with empty pockets besides.

He traded away the last thing he'd carried from home for coin enough to finally pay the cauldron's price, and walked out of the mine holding the first real prize of the whole campaign — then spent what was left seeing what the road still had for him. Traffic through the way station brought two men he had no place left to seat, and one old woman who wouldn't say what she actually wanted until she already had it — only that the land held another future worth knowing about, if he was willing to bargain for it. He took her on and sent the other two back the way they'd come — and let go, at last, of the quiet promise he'd folded into Fertile Valley's own ground a lifetime ago, since holding that ground had never once asked anything of him he wasn't already sworn to want.

## Turned back a second time

The ground under Corvin's feet still belonged to Perrin, and a hard look at what he actually had left to his name told him this wasn't the day that changed. Hesper talked him back from the mine's own garrison before he'd committed to anything real — one man thrown against four is a debt paid to nobody, not a fight worth having. Old Marta, asked what she made of the day, only allowed that some ground is worth walking away from twice.

He left the mine to Perrin a second time and turned instead to the road ahead, spending nearly everything he had left seeing what else the world still had to offer him. Of the three who answered the way station's call that day, he sent away the wolf-tamer and the quiet envoy both, and kept only the soldier who swore a plain thing: that any real victory, when Corvin finally won one, would pay for itself. There was no seat left at his own table for a third stranger until he did what Old Marta's own words had already been telling him to do — said her future out loud instead of keeping it folded away unspoken, the same lesson he'd taken as agreement without realizing it was meant for her too. Before he left the way station, he had the soldier's own commendations read aloud for anyone to hear, and spent the last of what he had there besides, banking the goodwill it bought rather than the coin itself, with a use for it already in mind.

## The banner, taken plainly

Two turns of quiet saving finally added up to enough — a banner nobody had actually defended since the day it was taken, and Corvin held just enough coin to take it in full view of everyone watching. Ressa's whole assessment fit in two lines: a debt paid in full is still a debt, whoever's holding it now, and she'd rather he took it plainly than pretend it was owed to him some other way.

He paid well above the People's Favor's own standing price and took it from Sorrel Wick outright, spending every coin he had to do it, then sent word of it ahead by his own soldier rather than let her find the empty space herself. He rode on to Charming Valley with nothing left in his purse, let Ressa show her own face at last for the goodwill it earned him there, and put the valley's own markets to work until the handful of idle men following him finally became a real company again.

## Charming Valley, undone in an afternoon

Lilianne walked into Charming Valley and took back in a single afternoon what had taken Corvin three long stretches to build — the banner, every man he'd just raised, gone in the same visit that cost Sorrel her own place among the Chancellor's own people. Hesper didn't waste breath mourning what was already lost — nothing left here worth the one treasure Corvin still carried, not with the Chancellor's own shadow still standing in the same yard.

He sent word to Sorrel that he'd noticed she'd already answered the day better than he had — then, with what daylight was left running short, let Hesper show her own face at last at Tinker's Fair and took the banner back from Sorrel that same afternoon she'd taken it back from him. He left before anyone else could get a hand on what little he had left, and found the pass itself standing in the way of anywhere further he meant to go.

## The pass that wouldn't let him further

The banner was gone again — Lilianne pried it loose the same week she'd taken it from him the first time — and word reached him that Sorrel had found something at Buried Giant worth more to her than anything he still carried.

He traded away the last whisper he kept right there at the pass to the dazzling woman who mans it, for whatever coin she'd give him, then walked on into Perrin's own valley without asking leave and spent the coin it bought him calling two fresh hands to his side. He sent Colm Rooke ahead of him besides — not for Perrin's sake alone, but because Sorrel had found a prize at Buried Giant that would end it for the rest of them the moment she woke to it again, and Perrin was the only one standing close enough to still do something about it before then.

## Everything the Chancellor could still take

The Chancellor had taken everything worth taking that week — the banner from him, then Sorrel's whole standing and her own prize besides — and there was no coin left in the world that could buy back a day like this one. She held the people's love in her fist now, out of anyone's reach, and no one who still had a stake in things pretended that wasn't going to be her day in the end — but building himself back into something that could stand still meant something, brick by brick, rather than waiting on the reckoning empty-handed.

He traded the last whisper he had left at the old bubbling lake — the ruined temple beside it wanted nothing to do with him — then walked on to the mine his people had once eyed and called two fresh hands to his side there, four standing behind him now instead of two, whatever that ended up being worth. He sent Colm Rooke ahead one more time, not with a warning this time, but to ask Perrin, plainly, for the answer about the key he was owed since the mill — in case there was no later day left to collect it.

## At the mill, past the lamp going out

Corvin went himself this time instead of sending Colm Rooke again, and didn't leave before Perrin finished the part of the letter he hadn't actually written down. Told him, unprompted, whose ring he'd actually been carrying all along — traded a plain answer for a plainer one back. Neither of them left before the lamp did. ## The Ruined Temple

Stood beside Perrin at the temple Sorrel picked herself, two months into the truce, and answered for the room before Perrin had finished getting to it — told her plainly there was space in Ashvale for her, same as there'd been for him, twice over, when he had nothing either. Took the cloth she finally held out — the first plain word either of them's had about Isolde in longer than he can count — and gave her the honest answer instead of a kind one: the debt isn't paid, but it isn't sitting unclaimed anymore. Let Perrin close the night out with the mill's own spare room and didn't argue the offer. Full exchange: `Game/Story/Conversation logs/oath-conversation-sorrel-perrin-corvin-R8-1.md`.

## Sorrel's letter

Read it twice before sitting down with it, the cloth already out of his coat before he'd decided to take it out. She'd taken back the tidy version she gave him at the Temple: Wren Sable's own box never called Isolde owed an apology, only that Wren never decided if she deserved one — meaning whatever she said aloud on the last day of her life at Drowned City may have been owed to *him*, not the other way round. Sorrel asked him plainly not to tidy that back up just because open is harder to carry than settled. Weighed the letter down on the mill table with the grain scale instead of folding it away, and said as much out loud, to Perrin or to no one. Means to go to Drowned City himself now — not to close the question, since she asked him not to, but because no Varn's actually stood on that ground since Isolde did, and he's done being the only one who's never gone to listen.

## Drowned City

A month's ride south of Ashvale, back across the Narrow Pass and into ground he'd never once crossed while it still answered to the Empire. Found the city itself nothing like the painting on his own wall — gulls instead of ghosts, no smoke anywhere he could find it — and found Lilianne of the Woods there besides, two days into some private errand of her own. Sat with her over tea rather than past history, and came away with the missing half of the debt: Isolde let Wren Sable live as a criminal for years rather than answer for a crime she'd actually committed herself, and only said so out loud the day she died. Didn't perform anger he didn't feel a right to. Told Lilianne plainly what Sorrel had said of her, and pointed her toward finding out herself whether it was still true. Left still wearing the ring and the cloth both, the question weighed down rather than folded shut, same as Sorrel asked.
